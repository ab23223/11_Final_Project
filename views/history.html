Climate Changes History

How has it changed and what effects has it had

Lets find out

1938 - Proof that global temperatures are rising

Steam engineer Callendar began collecting records from 147 weather stations across the world, after calculations he discovered that global temperatures had risen 0.3°C over the previous 50 years.

1958 - CO2 levels are rising

Postgraduate geochemist Charles David Keeling designed his own apparatus and went to a weather station ontop of the Mauna Loa volcano in Hawaii. Within five years he had provided the first unequivocal proof that CO2 concentrations were rising.

1969 - Earth’s temperature measured with satellites for first time

NASA’s Nimbus III satellite is launched into orbit – it provides the world’s first accurate measurement of global atmospheric temperatures. This provided an independent satellite record confirming that the Earth’s lower atmosphere was warming.

1991 - Wind power takes off in the UK

The UK’s first wind farm is constructed in Cornwall and it includes 10 turbines that together produce enough electricity for approximately 2,700 homes

1992 - Coral reefs at threat

Scientists realise that higher levels of CO2 in the ocean will make it harder for corals and other animals to build reefs

1997 - Hybrid cars invented

The first mass-market electric hybrid car is produced by Toyota

2007 - The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the rest of the planet

Polar regions are warming twice as fast as the rest of the Earth, putting polar bears and other wildlife at risk

2019 - Variety of life on Earth being lost at “unprecedented” pace

Up to one million animal and plant species are now threatened with extinction, and many could die out within decades

2021 - Many aspects of climate change are now inevitable and irreversible

Scientists predict the world will reach 1.5C of warming by 2040, earlier than initial warnings. That level of warming will lead to more heatwaves, intense storms, droughts and floods

Back on Land — Building a Sustainable Future

After over a century of industrial growth and rising emissions, we’ve reached a turning point. The choices we make today can still protect our planet for generations to come. Around the world, people are finding innovative ways to restore balance to life on land.

  • 🌳 Reforestation: Restoring forests and native vegetation to absorb carbon and protect wildlife.
  • ⚡ Clean Energy: Transitioning from coal and gas to renewable sources like wind, solar, and geothermal power.
  • 🏙️ Green Cities: Designing urban areas with electric transport, green roofs, and energy-efficient buildings.
  • 🚜 Sustainable Farming: Using regenerative agriculture to improve soil health and store carbon.
  • 🧠 Innovation: Developing technologies like carbon capture, recycling systems, and battery storage.
  • 🤝 Global Cooperation: Nations uniting under agreements like the Paris Accord to keep global warming below 1.5°C.

Learning From the Past

For decades, scientists and communities have tracked our planet’s changing climate. Those lessons form the foundation for our next steps. We now understand how human activity can reshape ecosystems — and how collective action can restore them.

History has taught us the cost of inaction, but it also shows that progress is possible when innovation meets determination.

What We Can Do

💡 How can individuals make a difference?
Every small choice adds up — reducing waste, conserving energy, using public transport, and supporting local sustainable brands all help lower emissions.
🌿 What are the biggest global priorities?
Ending deforestation, investing in renewables, and supporting developing nations in adapting to climate impacts are key to a balanced planet.
🌎 Can the future still be changed?
Yes — the future of our planet depends on what we do now. Climate change is a global challenge, but it’s also an opportunity to build a cleaner, fairer, and more sustainable world.
